ghana day 16

Posted on 2010-10-17 Edited on 2020-11-02

After a long week of work, we were hosted on Saturday to an amazing adventure in Ghana. Another bright and early 7 am start (we’ve determined no one sleeps in here) and a 2 hour bus ride took us to our turn off to the Amansuri Wetlands. Just another 5 km to go. But unlike the last 2 hours on some pretty good roads, our driver had to navigate a ‘road’ with our bus that should really only be walked, or driven with a 4 wheel drive hummer. Unbelievably, we made it the Amansuri wetlands.

Our first adventure was to get into small canoes with 4 or 5 per canoe. Ours was wooden, and had several small leaks in it, but we all had a seat., and a scoop that was used throughout the trip to bail out water as we travelled. Our boat ‘captain’ began moving us through the wetlands with a long stick that he pushed off from the bottom of the river. We travelled through this marshland, a river of reeds surrounded us, a single pathway of water that had been developed over years of use, much like a well worn path on a mountain trail. A bird sanctuary, we saw many birds and flowers. We moved from the wide open space of the river into a narrow channel and a rain forest. This was my favorite part of the journey, gliding through this mangrove forest. It was beautiful. The silence was broke by a canoe coming the other way, filled with a group signing an African song … we past each with high fives, and their singing soon disappeared. Emerging from the forest, the wetland opened up to a wide river. The long stick was now exchanged for paddles, we took turns helping paddle our canoe. The trip in the canoe took an hour. Of course, it was 30 degrees, but some cloud cover and a little rain along the way made it comfortable, I think I’m actually getting used to this humidity here!


Our journey then took us to the village of Nzulezo. Only reachable by water, and only by canoe for those who lived here, the village is built on stilts and rests entirely above water. 450 people live in this village. We got out of our canoes and were welcomed by the locals, who walked us down the ‘main street’ and throught the town to the far end of the village. I have been lucky to have explored some interesting parts of the world, but this moment, at this place, is the most fascinating I have ever seen. The town was built of wood, alot of bamboo, some rubber plants, extended over the water on stilts. Small rooms made up the houses of the people here. We could take pictures of the town, but needed to ask permission to take a picture of the people first. There was a small market, and a one room school house for the kids up to grade 6. The inhibitants fished and farmed, and everything else they needed must be brought by boat. They had no electricity, a generator provided the only power they had in the village.

We met with the town chief, who explained the history of the village in an African language, that was translated by our guide for us. We then offered any gifts we had for the school children, I had brought several pairs of childrens sandalls with me on the journey that we presented to the chief. many of us bought a small wood carved canoe to thank them for their hospitality.

We expored the village, which included a small farm with goats and chickens, built on stilts over the water like the rest of the village. They had 3 churches for this small community. We then headed back in the canoe for our hour long trip back, it was great to get to experience the beauty of the wetlands again one more time.

Our trip ended with some time to hang out at the beach, as the start of the wetlands was located right at the coast. I explored the shore line and collected a few beautuful sea shells.

An amazing day.
