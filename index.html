comparison between the privacy preserving mechanisms used in Hyperledger Zeto and Consensys Anonymous Zether

Posted on 2024-08-14

The comparison between the privacy-preserving mechanisms used in Hyperledger Zeto and ConsenSys’s Anonymous Zether, particularly in the context of CBDCs with privacy transactions and non-repudiation, involves evaluating the underlying cryptographic methods, scalability, and the ability to maintain privacy while providing non-repudiation.

Overview of the Two Systems

1. Hyperledger Zeto

  • Cryptographic Basis: Zeto primarily utilizes zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) and encryption techniques to ensure privacy. The zeto_anon_enc_nullifier.sol contract, for example, focuses on anonymous transactions where transaction details are not directly revealed.
  • Privacy Mechanisms: Zeto relies on ZKPs for both proving the validity of transactions and ensuring that no double-spending occurs without revealing transaction details.
  • Non-Repudiation: Zeto’s design does not inherently include non-repudiation mechanisms as it emphasizes privacy, but non-repudiation can be added using commitment schemes or arbitration protocols.

    2. ConsenSys Anonymous Zether

  • Cryptographic Basis: Zether uses homomorphic encryption, particularly leveraging the ElGamal encryption scheme. Homomorphic properties allow the system to perform operations on encrypted data without revealing it.
  • Privacy Mechanisms: Zether extends the standard ElGamal encryption by integrating zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) to verify that transactions are valid (e.g., ensuring sufficient balance) without revealing the actual amounts or parties involved.
  • Non-Repudiation: Similar to Zeto, Zether focuses on privacy. However, non-repudiation in the Zether protocol would require external mechanisms since homomorphic encryption does not inherently provide non-repudiation.

Comparison for CBDC Use Case

Privacy Protection

  • Zeto: Zeto provides strong privacy through the use of ZKPs. It ensures that no transaction details are exposed on-chain, making it very suitable for CBDCs where transaction privacy is a priority.
  • Zether: Zether also provides robust privacy by ensuring that transactions remain confidential through homomorphic encryption and ZKPs. The privacy guarantees are similar to Zeto but rely on different cryptographic foundations.

Scalability and Efficiency

  • Zeto: Zeto’s reliance on ZKPs can be computationally intensive, particularly for large-scale transaction volumes typical of CBDCs. However, optimizations in ZKP techniques (e.g., zk-SNARKs) can mitigate this.
  • Zether: Zether’s use of homomorphic encryption, combined with ZKPs, provides a scalable solution that allows for operations on encrypted data without revealing it. The homomorphic properties can sometimes offer more efficient transactions compared to pure ZKP-based systems.

Non-Repudiation

  • Zeto: Non-repudiation would need to be added via additional layers, such as commitment schemes or digital signatures. Zeto’s architecture does not inherently provide non-repudiation, but these mechanisms can be integrated effectively.
  • Zether: Similarly, Zether would require additional mechanisms for non-repudiation. The homomorphic encryption scheme used does not natively support non-repudiation, so solutions like commitment schemes, digital signatures, or arbitration protocols would need to be incorporated.

    Achieving Non-Repudiation

1. Commitment and Reveal Schemes

  • Both systems could implement a commitment and reveal scheme where parties commit to transaction details in a hashed form before the transaction. If a dispute arises, the parties can reveal their commitments to an arbiter or smart contract to prove the transaction’s authenticity.

    2. Digital Signatures

  • Adding digital signatures to both Zeto and Zether transactions can ensure non-repudiation. Each transaction could be signed by the parties involved, creating a verifiable record that the transaction was initiated by the respective parties.

    3. Trusted Third-Party Arbitration

  • Both systems could incorporate a trusted third-party (TTP) or smart contract-based arbitration mechanism. If a dispute arises, the TTP can decrypt and verify the transaction details (under specific conditions) without exposing them publicly.

    4. Non-Repudiation Tokens

  • Issuing non-repudiation tokens post-transaction can be another approach. These tokens serve as proof that both parties acknowledged the transaction. In case of a dispute, these tokens can be presented as evidence.

    Conclusion: Which Is Better for CBDC?

    • Privacy: Both Zeto and Zether provide excellent privacy guarantees, making either suitable for a privacy-focused CBDC. However, Zether’s use of homomorphic encryption combined with ZKPs offers a slightly different approach that might be more flexible in certain scenarios.
    • Scalability: Zether might have a slight edge in scalability due to the efficiency of homomorphic encryption, though Zeto’s reliance on advanced ZKPs also makes it a strong contender.
    • Non-Repudiation: Neither system natively supports non-repudiation. However, Zether’s framework might be easier to extend with non-repudiation mechanisms due to its cryptographic flexibility.

Best Solution for CBDC: If scalability and cryptographic flexibility are priorities alongside privacy, Zether might be the better choice for a CBDC. However, for systems that heavily rely on zk-SNARKs and other zero-knowledge techniques, Zeto is also a strong candidate. The choice ultimately depends on the specific requirements of the CBDC system, particularly in how it needs to balance privacy, efficiency, and non-repudiation.

To implement non-repudiation effectively, additional mechanisms like digital signatures, commitment schemes, or trusted arbitration need to be integrated into either system.
